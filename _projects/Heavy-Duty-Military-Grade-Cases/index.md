## Working with foam
I spent two years working in the foam conversion industry at UK Foam Converter. Starting on the workshop floor, I operated a range of Computer Numerical Control (CNC) machines, gaining hands-on experience in both the machining process and the behaviour of different foam materials under manufacturing conditions.
This experience taught me that successful machining depends on understanding a wide range of factors, including wall thicknesses, tooling marks, swarf management, material movement on the vacuum bed, and delamination. Small changes to cutting parameters or component geometry could often be the difference between a successful part and a failed one.
Working directly with production equipment gave me a strong appreciation of Design for Manufacture (DfM), helping me understand how design decisions influence machining efficiency, part quality, and repeatability.

## Pockets to contain
{% include image-gallery.html images="project2.jpg" height="400" %}

Positioning the largest component within the available case volume is typically the key driver for the overall insert layout. Once the primary item has been established, the location and orientation of accessory pockets can be optimised to maximise space utilisation, maintain structural integrity, and improve the user experience when packing and unpacking equipment.


## Contraints of internal volume

{% include image-gallery.html images="EndviewBase.png" height="400" %}

Injection-moulded cases often reduce the available internal volume due to draft angles, corner radii, ribs, and other moulded features. Additional case features, such as wheels, handles, hinges, and reinforcement structures, should also be considered during the insert design process as they can further impact the usable space. Where possible, obtaining the manufacturer's internal case model is the preferred approach. Using the case interior geometry as the foundation for the foam insert ensures that the available volume is accurately defined by the case manufacturer, reducing the risk of fitment issues and helping to maximise the usable storage space.

## Good File Management
{% include image-gallery.html images="KeepingOrganised.png" height="400" %}

No matter how complete a design appears, changes are almost always required in the future. Maintaining a well-structured CAD model makes those revisions faster, easier, and less prone to error.
Good file management can be as simple as grouping related features together in the feature tree to create a logical workflow. For more complex designs, individual features can be renamed to clearly describe their purpose, making the model easier to understand for both the original designer and anyone who may work on it later.
A well-organised model improves maintainability, reduces the time required to implement design changes, and helps ensure design intent is clearly communicated throughout the product lifecycle.

The few minutes spent organising a model during creation can save hours of rework when modifications are required months or even years later.
